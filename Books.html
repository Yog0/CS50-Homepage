Books

This is an incredible book, and one I'd recommend to anyone unreservedly. Frankl is a Holocaust survivor who spent time in multiple Nazi concentration camps through World War 2. He gives a very simple history of that time - descriptions of the deaths of his friends are horrific, but almost matter-of-fact. He uses this to illustrate the central concept of his philosophy: that we can't change what happens around us, but we can choose how to react to it. Our primary drive is not pleasure, but the search for meaning.

Yuval Noah Harari's Sapiens is one of the most successful and influential popular science books of the last decade. Harari's style is simple and engaging, and provides simple explanations for the rise of our species to global dominance. A major part of this is that we have the ability to create a shared reality from things that don't actually physically exist - think money, religion, or human rights. This allows us to cooperate with total strangers to a shared end - something impossible in other animals. This book was extremely influential, and frequently made me reconsider my assumptions about the world.

Slaughterhouse-Five is a classic. It reads like nothing else. The book has a semi-autographical aspect that deals with the bombing of Dresden in World War 2, combined with sci-fi existentialism in Vonnegut's distinctive style. Just read it.
